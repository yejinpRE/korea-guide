00
The practical bits

Before You Go입국·교통·앱·결제 — 도착 전에 알아둘 것

Boring but essential. Sort these before you land and the rest of the trip runs itself.

Arrival

Complete the Q-CODE online before landing (health declaration, free). Do it 24–72h before the flight.

From Incheon (ICN) to central Seoul: AREX express (~43 min, ~9,500 KRW) or the cheaper all-stop.

Getting Around

Seoul → Busan: KTX high-speed train, ~2.5h. Bookable about 1 month ahead on letskorail.com or the Korail Talk app.

A Korail Pass (3 or 5 days) only pays off if you're doing the full appendix loop — for Seoul+Busan, single tickets are simpler.

Payments / T-money

Card and phone pay work almost everywhere — you'll barely touch cash. The one catch: topping up a T-money card is cash only.

Grab a T-money card at any CU, GS25 or 7-Eleven (2,500 KRW + top-up) and load it with cash there. Works on metro, bus and taxi. Keep a little cash for top-ups and traditional markets; "Global" ATMs at 7-Eleven if you run out.

Essential Apps

Naver Map / Kakao Map — Google Maps barely works here. You can book restaurants right inside Naver Map.

Catch Table / Tabling for the good restaurants (they always have long lines). Kakao Map for reliable reviews.

KakaoTaxi (Uber equivalent), Korail Talk (trains).

Delivery magic

Baemin / Coupang Eats — you can literally get food delivered anywhere, 24h. At 3am, on a bridge, in the middle of a park. Any food, any drinks.

Electricity

220V, Type C/F plugs — same as Italy. No adapter needed.

01
How to read this city

Meet Seoul서울을 동네로 읽는 법

Seoul isn't really a checklist of landmarks — it's a collection of neighbourhoods, each with its own mood. The same city feels completely different depending on which one you're standing in. So this guide is built by lifestyle, not by district: where to go for design and trends, for old hanok streets, for the river, for a quiet international afternoon.

Go by neighbourhood동네로 보기

Each chapter ahead is a different side of Seoul. You don't need to do them all — pick the moods that fit your friend and string those days together.

The chapters

Heritage & Design — palaces, hanok, the best food day.
Creative — Seongsu, Euljiro, Hongdae for trends & cafés.
Uptown — Apgujeong & COEX, the polished Gangnam side.
Eclectic — Hannam, Itaewon & Yongsan, global and mixed.
River — the Han, decoded.

Ease into day one첫날은 가볍게

You land around midday, drop your bags, and you'll be more tired than you think. Don't over-plan the first day — pick one neighbourhood, eat something great, catch a sunset, and let the jet lag settle.

Yejin's tip Day one is for arriving, not conquering. Save the big walking days for when you've slept.

If you remember one thing딱 하나만 기억하면

When something is famous but feels like it's "for tourists," there's almost always a better local version one neighbourhood over. I'll flag those as we go — the "go here instead" notes are where the real Seoul is.

02
My favourite day

Heritage & Design전통과 현대가 한 골목에 — 내가 제일 좋아하는 동네

My favourite area, and the heart of old Seoul — all in Jongno-gu. Palaces and hanok alleys, with trendy cafés, galleries and great food tucked between the old buildings. The neighbourhoods sit around Gyeongbokgung Palace, so you can string them together on foot. One day honestly isn't enough.

The palaces궁궐 · 도심의 중심

Seoul has five grand Joseon-dynasty palaces, but two do the job: Gyeongbokgung, the big formal one, and Changdeokgung, the quieter one with the famous garden. They're a 15-minute walk apart, with the hanok neighbourhoods in between.

Gyeongbokgung · the big one

The first and largest of the five palaces. King Sejong invented Hangul, the Korean alphabet, here.

1395Built as the Joseon dynasty's main palace
1592Burned down in the Japanese invasion; left in ruins ~270 years
1910–45Colonial rule — over 90% of its buildings demolished
to 2045Slowly being rebuilt from historical records
Do: Changing of the Guard at the main gate (~10am & 2pm). Free entry in hanbok.

Changdeokgung · UNESCO · the garden one

The best-preserved palace, built to follow the natural hills rather than impose symmetry. Its "Secret Garden" was the royals' private retreat — about 60% of the grounds.

1405Built as the "eastern palace"
1997Named a UNESCO World Heritage Site
Do: book the Secret Garden tour ahead — limited tickets, English tours sell out (best in autumn).
Yejin's take

Pick one big palace (Gyeongbokgung) and one garden (Changdeokgung), then spend the rest of the day in the neighbourhoods around them rather than ticking off all five. Palaces are closed Mondays (Changdeokgung) / Tuesdays (Gyeongbokgung), so check before you go.

Seochon서촌

West of Gyeongbokgung. Quiet hanok lanes with bookshops, small galleries and calm cafés — more local and low-key than Bukchon.

Bukchon · Samcheong북촌 · 삼청동

East of the palace. The postcard hanok village, with the Samcheong-dong café and gallery street running off it. Busier and prettier — come early for the quiet lanes.

Ikseon-dong · Insadong익선동 · 인사동

Narrow hanok lanes brought back to life as restaurants and bars (Ikseon), next to the traditional crafts and tea of Insadong. The best stretch for dinner and drinks. Don't miss Jogyesa Temple nearby — I love it.

Gwanghwamun · Cheonggyecheon광화문 · 청계천

The civic heart of the city, and very walkable — easy to reach from anywhere. If you want to take in the Cheonggyecheon stream, my route: start at Jonggak Station, see Jogyesa Temple nearby, then walk down along the stream toward Gwanghwamun Plaza. Keep going and you can walk all the way to Deoksugung or even Gyeongbokgung — though in summer that's a lot of heat, so judge the day.

In summer there's a ground fountain in Gwanghwamun Plaza, usually full of kids running through the water. And the view of Gyeongbokgung from the plaza is really something.

A bit of history

That spot has a story: during the Japanese occupation the colonial Government-General building stood here, deliberately blocking the palace. It was demolished in the 1990s, and a giant statue of Admiral Yi Sun-sin now stands on the plaza — the legendary general who defeated the Japanese navy in battle after battle.

Yejin's tip On a Monday, walk the Cheonggyecheon stream up to Gwanghwamun — you'll see all the office workers out and about. It's a very Seoul scene.

Up the hill인왕산 · 북악스카이웨이

Inwangsan hike — right behind Seochon, an hour up the old city wall and the whole city opens up below you. One of the easiest "Mountain Seoul" hits, straight from the neighbourhood.

Bugak Skyway drive — if hiking's not your thing, the road that winds over Bugaksan gives you the same city-from-above view from the car, with lookout stops along the way. A lovely one at sunset.

Galleries갤러리

This area is dense with galleries — from big institutions to tiny hanok spaces. Start with the MMCA (National Museum of Modern & Contemporary Art), right by the palaces.

03
The trendy side

Creative Seoul트렌디한 서울 — 패션·디자인·카페가 매번 바뀌는 곳

If someone asked me where to feel modern Seoul, this would be my answer. Fashion, design, pop-ups, cafés, galleries and trends that change every time I visit. The fun isn't a checklist — it's wandering and seeing what you find.

Seongsu성수

If you like fashion, beauty, cafés or design, plan to spend much longer here than you expect. It's bigger than it looks — flagship stores, pop-ups and brand experiences, with new exhibitions every season. Huge Olive Young, Tamburins, Matin Kim, Gentle Monster…

If you just want the main strip, start from Dior Seongsu and walk Yeonmujang-gil (연무장길) — but don't stay on the main street. The best cafés hide in the smaller alleys.

Grab a coffee and walk over to Seoul Forest — locals reading, picnicking, walking dogs on the grass. The brand flagships here are destinations in themselves, half store, half art installation — and if your friend likes K-pop, SM Entertainment's HQ and store are right here.
Brand flagships — Google Maps ◍House of Nowhere, Seoul — Gentle Monster's futuristic flagship↗ ◍Dior Seongsu — started as a pop-up, became so iconic it stayed↗ ◍KWANGYA @ SEOUL — SM Entertainment HQ & flagship store, Korea's first & biggest K-pop label↗

Euljiro을지로

One of my favourite neighbourhoods. It used to be full of small manufacturing — printing shops, electronics and instrument arcades, metal workshops. As those spaces got reused as bars and cafés, it slowly turned hip, and now old-timers' hangouts and young crowds mix in the same alleys to make a really particular atmosphere. It's a bit like the cyberpunk version of Korea that foreigners imagine — old-school and trendy, side by side.

Izakayas, wine bars, cocktail bars, old-school nopo diners — it's all jumbled together, so you can do whatever you're in the mood for. Duck into a no-frills nopo for cheap draft beer, or hunt down a hidden cocktail bar. It completely changes character at night.

Getting there

Euljiro 3-ga station, or walk from Jonggak along the Cheonggyecheon stream. Heads up: the good places need reservations — the best ones are booked out weeks, even a month, ahead.

If someone asked me — Myeongdong or Euljiro for the evening? — I'd choose Euljiro almost every time.

Hongdae & Yeonnam홍대 · 연남

Hongdae draws tourists and young Koreans alike, but it's a huge commercial district — you have to find the right pocket and dodge the tourist traps. The main streets are packed with big flagships (Apple, LINE Store and the like), but duck into the alleys and the mood flips completely. Compared to the more polished areas in this guide, Hongdae is younger, more casual, more unfiltered.

It grew up around an underground music scene, so there are lots of small live venues and music-related spaces — worth seeking out if that's your thing. (The Hongdae RYSE Hotel is a fun base, too.)

Honestly, the parts Koreans love more are the edges — Yeonnam, Yeonhui, Mangwon (and Seongsu) — where small independent cafés, little drinking spots and vintage shops hide in the side streets. Not super polished, but there's an older, authentic, genuine-culture feeling to them.

Yejin's tip Don't try to hit every famous café you saw on Instagram. Half the fun is walking around and stumbling onto places you weren't looking for.

Yeonnam, up close연남 골목

My own way to do Yeonnam: walk the Gyeongui Line Forest Park — an old railway turned into a long green strip — and duck deep into the side alleys off it, where the pretty cafés and cool little restaurants hide. There's honestly so much here that recommending feels hard, and half the joy is the small shops and bookstores you find along the way.

One quirk: this area has a ton of izakaya. If you want a Japanese-style pub (much cheaper and much tastier than in the UK), this is the place.

04
Polished & grown-up · Gangnam

Uptown Seoul강남 — 럭셔리하고 세련된 결

Gangnam gets recommended a lot, but the area around Gangnam Station itself is just huge commercial and office blocks. The good parts are uptown: the luxury, polished, grown-up side of the city.

Apgujeong · Sinsa압구정 · 신사

Skip Gangnam Station. The good part of Gangnam is up here — still Gangnam, but the luxury, trendy side. The spine of it is Dosan-daero (도산대로), the boulevard running through Apgujeong and Sinsa, lined with flagship boutiques, concept stores and some of the city's best high-end dining and cafés.

Compared to Seongsu, it's more luxurious and a bit more grown-up. Seongsu for youth and energy, this for polish.
The three streets

Dosan-daero — the main axis: luxury flagships, designer concept stores, high-end restaurants.
Garosu-gil — the tree-lined shopping street: fashion and cafés.
Apgujeong Rodeo — the trendier pocket. A spa treatment too, if you're in the mood.

Landmark to orient by — Google Maps ◍HAUS NOWHERE Dosan — worth a look itself, and a handy landmark — the hottest spots cluster around here↗

Gangnam Station & COEX강남역 · 코엑스

When people say "Gangnam," they often mean the area around Gangnam Station — but that's really just an express-bus hub and office district. Lots of people, not much for a traveller to see. Honestly, you can skip it.

COEX is the part that's worth it — a huge mall complex with the photogenic Starfield Library (Byeolmadang), an aquarium and SM Town. And not far away is Lotte World Tower in Jamsil — Korea's tallest building, with an observation deck and a theme park at its base.
Yejin's tip On a hot summer day, this is a good indoor escape — wander the Starfield Library and grab a coffee at the % Arabica inside.
05
Where the world mixes in · Yongsan

Eclectic Seoul용산 — 한남·해방촌·이태원·삼각지

If one word has to cover Yongsan, "eclectic" is it. This is where you'll find Seoul's most expensive apartments and the National Museum of Korea; its most global neighbourhood and its most chaotic nightlife; quiet hilltop bars and the trendiest dining streets — all packed into one district by the river. These pockets sit close together, so they string into one easy day (and a long night).

Hannam한남동

Toward the river it's expensive apartments and embassies; around the station there's a proper fashion street. You'll find the Korean labels the stylish crowd actually wears — Ohlssoo, RECTO, Le September, ron ron and plenty more — plus fun concept stores. Seriously stylish crowd, you might even spot a celebrity. Try not to feel underdressed.

The Lemaire flagship is worth a look too — the Paris label that Koreans and Japanese love, and (apart from Paris) its only flagships are said to be here and in Tokyo. A very cool space. The whole area is polished and effortlessly stylish — use the Hyundai Card Music/Design Library as a landmark, wander the back alleys for great little restaurants, and take it slow.

Spots — Google Maps ◍Lemaire Hannam flagship — Paris label, a flagship only here & Tokyo↗ ◍Gourmet 494, Naine One Hannam — food hall, a cool indoor break on a hot day↗
Yejin's tip The real fun here is treasure-hunting the back alleys — there's so much tucked away. (Just pace yourself, it's summer.) If the heat gets to you, duck into the Gourmet 494 food hall at Naine One Hannam for a bite.

Itaewon & Haebangchon이태원 · 해방촌

One of the most unusual areas in Korea. A US army base used to sit here, so it grew up global — and along with Hongdae, it's where the most foreigners live in Seoul. Naturally a club culture grew with it, so this is house- and techno-club territory.

At night it gets genuinely chaotic, but it's also where you'll find the city's real diversity (lots of gay clubs), and tucked inside it are small, niche spaces that are completely your own. It's a "you see as much as you know" kind of place — the more you understand it, the better it gets.

How I'd do it

This is where I've spent a lot of nights out for house music — there are loads of clubs, and the fun is in the back alleys, not the main strip. Honestly the main Itaewon drag is too hectic — I'd soak up the atmosphere, then slip off toward Hannam or up to Haebangchon.

My picks — Google Maps ◍teller — my favourite house-music venue↗ ◍kamar — a friend's place, delicious, friendly & comfy↗

Samgakji삼각지

Grown up around Yongsan KTX station, this pocket really took off once big names moved in — Amorepacific (one of the giants behind K-beauty) built its HQ here, and HYBE (BTS's label) too. With them came a wave of small independent spaces.

It's now a fun mix of old-school diners and hip new spots — brunch places, natural-wine bars, tiny independent shops, including spots run by winners of that Netflix cooking-competition show. Another "you see as much as you know" pocket. A natural dinner stop after a Han River sunset nearby.

Landmark — Google Maps ◍Amorepacific HQ — striking building, open public spaces inside↗

National Museum of Korea국립중앙박물관

Also in Yongsan, and worth the trip — a huge, beautifully laid-out museum tracing Korean history from prehistory to the Joseon dynasty. The grounds and reflecting pond are lovely too, and entry to the permanent galleries is free.

Yejin's tip A perfect hot-day option — it's big, cool and indoors, easily a half-day.

06
Not all the same river

River Seoul한강은 다 같은 한강이 아니다

The Han River is loooong — like the Thames. You get a completely different mood depending on where along it you go. Most tourists default to Yeouido; here's how the stretches actually differ.

The Han, decoded한강, 결 나누기

Nodeul Island is a small island sitting between the bridges — usually a nice spot for performances, a bookshop and a sunset over the water, though parts are under construction right now, so check before you go. Whichever stretch you pick, the move is the same: grab food and watch the sun go down over the river. Each part of the Han has its own mood — choose the one that fits your evening.

The move on a nice evening: order delivery food to the park (yes, you can), spread out on the grass, watch the sun go down over the water. Very Seoul.
Pick your stretch
Nodeul 노들섬

A small island between the bridges — performances, a bookshop, a lovely sunset. Partly under construction now, so check first.

Yeouido 여의도

The big one. Wide riverside park, cherry blossoms in spring, the default picnic spot.

Mangwon 망원

The west-side, local pick — grab food off Mangri-dan-gil or in Yeonnam and bring it down. Younger, neighbourhood feel.

Banpo 반포

Rainbow fountain off the bridge at night, big lawns — the classic chicken-and-beer picnic.

Ttukseom 뚝섬

By Seoul Forest & Seongsu. Tent zones, runners, pools in summer — the active, younger stretch.

Ichon 이촌

Quiet and local — a calm walking-and-running stretch with almost no tourists.

Spot — Google Maps ◍Nodeul Island — check ahead, partly under construction↗

Yeouido여의도

Korea's finance island — home to the major securities firms and the National Assembly, whose green-domed roof you'll spot from the main boulevard. Lately it's gone from all-business to genuinely lively: The Hyundai Seoul turned it into a retail destination, the aging office towers are being actively redeveloped, and more is coming (an opera house among them). It only gets better from here.

There's a lot of green space too, so the rhythm flips with the week — finance crowds on weekdays, families coming out to play on weekends. The 63 Building now houses Centre Pompidou Hanwha, the French museum's first Korean outpost (opened June 2026), plus a revamped sky observatory; in spring, the cherry blossoms along Yunjung-ro are famous.

Worth a stop at The Hyundai Seoul: the floor that connects to Yeouido Station is full of trendy brands and fun pop-ups, and the F&B floor feels like someone gathered great little hole-in-the-wall restaurants from all over the city into one department store. Grab a dessert and coffee there and take it down to the river park for a picnic.

Spots — Google Maps ◍The Hyundai Seoul — trendy brands, pop-ups & a great food floor↗ ◍Yeouido Hangang Park — Mulbit Plaza, for the picnic↗ ◍Hanwha 63 Building — Centre Pompidou Hanwha + sky observatory (link coming)↗
Yejin's tip My own hidden favourite is the Saetgang Ecological Park under Saetgang Bridge — it's genuinely beautiful, and renting a bike to ride from here along the water to the other Han River parks is just the best. The bridges off Yeouido (Mapo, Seogang, Wonhyo, Hangang) are fun to cross too, on foot or by bus — you get the river and the skyline the whole way over.

Sea, hills & seafood · 3 nights

Busan부산 — 바다와 언덕, 그리고 회

KTX gets you here from Seoul in about 2.5 hours. A looser, sunnier, seaside counterpart to Seoul — beaches, a hillside art village, the country's biggest fish market, and a coastal train ride.

Arrival & Gwangalli도착 · 광안리

Settle into your hotel neighbourhood and walk Jeonpo Café Street. In the evening, Gwangalli Beach — the bridge lights up and the bars line the sand.

Do this

Café-hop in Jeonpo, then beachfront drinks at Gwangalli after dark.

Gamcheon & Jagalchi감천 · 자갈치

The postcard day. Gamcheon Culture Village tumbling down the hillside in pastel, then down to the water for the markets and the coast.

Do this

Gamcheon Culture Village → BIFF Square for street food → Jagalchi fish market → Jeoryeong coastal trail → Huinnyeoul Culture Village along the cliffs.

Haeundae & Sky Capsule해운대 · 스카이캡슐

Haedong Yonggungsa — a rare temple right on the sea — then the Haeundae neighbourhood and beach. From Mipo Station, ride the Sky Capsule or the Blue Line train along the coast.

Book ahead

The Sky Capsule sells out — book about 1 month in advance, ideally a sunset slot.

Honest takes on the famous ones

Tourist Classics정석 관광지 — 솔직하게

Famous doesn't always mean bad. Here are the postcard spots, rated honestly — the ones worth it, and the ones you can happily skip. Whether they're worth a detour depends on how much time you have and whether it's your friend's first time in Korea.

Namsan · N Seoul Tower남산 · 갈 만함

A classic, but a good one. The sunset over the city really is lovely, and you won't regret going up. The tower deck itself is fine — the nicer move is the rooftop cafés and bars right nearby. The fences up top are covered in "love locks" — couples leave one with the superstition that it means they'll never break up.

Do this

Go for sunset, skip the deck queue, settle into a rooftop with a view instead.

Spot — Google Maps ◍Rooftop near Namsan↗

Myeongdong명동 · 스킵 가능

Top of every guidebook, but honestly Koreans don't really go here — it's cosmetics shops and tourist crowds. Not bad, just not where the city actually lives.

If it's right by your hotel, take a quick look. For the evening, go to Euljiro instead — or Jongno 3-ga, or Hannam. That's where it gets good.

If you have a spare day

Day Trips서울에서 당일치기

Optional add-ons from Seoul if the pace allows.

DMZ반나절 · 종일

The border tour — sobering and unlike anything else. A full day once you factor in the meet-up, travel and checkpoints. Book the JSA tour 6–8 weeks ahead, it fills up fast.

Jeonju전주

Korea's hanok town and food capital — and closer than it sounds. By KTX it's only about 1.5–2 hours from Seoul, so it works as a day trip (similar total time to a DMZ tour), though it's lovely enough to stay a night if you can.

Do this

Hanok Village, Gyeonggijeon Shrine, Jaman Mural Village, and Nambu Market for the food. Bibimbap was basically born here.

Optional traditional extension

Gyeongju & Andong전통을 더 보고 싶다면 — 경주·안동

Not part of the core 10 days. But if your friend wants the deeper, older Korea, Gyeongju and Andong are the real thing — ancient Silla and Confucian heritage. Gyeongju slots onto the Seoul→Busan line; Andong is an easy add for folk-village culture. Add a night or two.

Gyeongju경주 · 약 1–2박

The ancient Silla capital — an open-air museum of royal tombs and temples. Easy train onward to Busan (~1h), so it fits between Seoul and the coast.

Do this

Hwangnidan-gil, Daereungwon tombs, Cheomseongdae, Donggung Palace lit up at night. Then Bulguksa + Seokguram.

Andong안동 · 약 1박

The home of Korean Confucian and folk tradition. The Hahoe Folk Village is a living village, not a museum set — real families still live in the hanok.

Do this

Hahoe Folk Village (mask-dance performance on Wednesdays), the Mask Museum, and Andong's famous jjimdak (braised chicken).
